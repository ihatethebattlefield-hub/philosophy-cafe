The Ethics Dilemma

A thought-experiment on right action
Thought Experiment
The Scenario

A runaway trolley races toward five workers pinned on the track. You stand by a lever. Pull it, and the trolley diverts onto a side line — where one worker is standing. Do you pull the lever, sacrificing one to save five?

Now a harder version: there is no lever. A large stranger stands beside you on a bridge. Push him onto the track and his body stops the trolley, saving the five — but he dies. Same arithmetic, different act. Does it feel the same? Why?

The Exercise
  • The Utilitarian reply: The greatest good for the greatest number. The math seems clear — but where does it fail?
  • The Deontological reply: Kant would say you must never use a person merely as a means. Pushing the stranger violates a rule no outcome can justify.
  • Your intuition: Which version troubles you more, and what does that reveal about the principle you actually live by?
  • The edge: Change one detail — the five are strangers, the one is your child. Does the "right" answer survive?
A Line to Carry
"Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law." — Immanuel Kant, Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals
