Arthur Schopenhauer

1788 – 1860 · Danzig & Frankfurt
Pessimism & Post-Kantian Idealism
Biography

Arthur Schopenhauer was the philosopher of pessimism, a man whose acerbic temperament matched his dark vision of life. Born into a wealthy merchant family, he abandoned commerce for philosophy, studying at Göttingen and Berlin.

His magnum opus, "The World as Will and Representation," appeared when he was thirty. It argued that behind the veil of appearances lies a blind, striving will that perpetuates desire and suffering. For decades his work was ignored; he finally gained recognition only in old age.

He influenced Nietzsche, Wagner, Freud, Wittgenstein, and generations of artists. His prose is sharp, his judgments merciless, and his compassion for animals unexpectedly tender.

Great Ideas
  • The World as Will: Beneath the world of representation is a single, purposeless will driving all life.
  • Pessimism: Life oscillates between pain and boredom; satisfaction is fleeting because desire is endless.
  • Aesthetic Contemplation: Art temporarily liberates us from the will by allowing pure, disinterested perception of Platonic Ideas.
  • Compassion: Recognizing the one will in all beings grounds genuine morality and respect for animals.
  • Denial of the Will: The saint and the ascetic achieve a kind of salvation by quieting the will's demands.
In His Words
"Compassion is the basis of morality." — Schopenhauer
