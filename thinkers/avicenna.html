Avicenna (Ibn Sina)

980 – 1037 · Persia & Central Asia
Islamic Peripatetic Philosophy
Biography

Ibn Sina, known in the Latin West as Avicenna, was a physician, scientist, and philosopher whose influence stretched from Cairo to Paris. Born near Bukhara, he memorized the Quran by age ten and mastered medicine, mathematics, and law while still a youth.

His "Canon of Medicine" remained a standard textbook in Europe for centuries, while his philosophical encyclopedia "The Healing" synthesized Aristotle, Neoplatonism, and Islamic thought. He wrote under court patronage, in prison, and in hiding, producing a corpus of extraordinary range.

Avicenna is often credited with reintroducing rigorous metaphysics into the Islamic world and medieval Europe. His distinction between essence and existence became a cornerstone of later scholastic theology.

Great Ideas
  • The Flying Man: Imagine a person suspended in empty space, unaware of body or world. Awareness of self would still remain — proof that the soul knows itself directly.
  • Necessary Existent: God alone is necessary in Himself; all else is contingent and depends for its existence upon the Necessary.
  • Essence & Existence: In creatures, what a thing is and that it is are distinct; in God alone do essence and existence coincide.
  • The Active Intellect: A single transcendent intellect illuminates human minds, enabling us to grasp universal knowledge.
  • Prophetic Knowledge: The prophet receives imaginative and intellectual truths in a vivid, symbolic form accessible to the community.
In His Words
"The knowledge of anything, since all things have causes, is not acquired or complete unless it is known by its causes." — Avicenna
"I prefer a short life with width to a narrow one with length." — Avicenna
"The world is divided into men who have wit and no religion and men who have religion and no wit." — Avicenna
