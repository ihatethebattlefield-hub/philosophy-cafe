Laozi

c. 6th century BCE · Chu (China)
Daoism (Taoism)
Biography

Legend holds that Laozi — "the Old Master" — was a keeper of archives at the Zhou court who, disillusioned by the decadence of the age, rode west on a water buffalo. At the border, a guard begged him to leave behind his wisdom; the result was the Dao De Jing, a slender book of eighty-one enigmatic verses.

Whether he was one man or a name for many voices, Laozi stands as the fountainhead of Daoism — a current of thought that flows against striving, naming, and control, urging instead a return to the spontaneous rhythm of the Dao, the Way.

Where Confucius sought to order the world through ritual, Laozi invited us to yield like water, to act without forcing, and to find the great in the small.

Great Ideas
  • The Dao (Way): The nameless source and pattern of all things, utterly beyond full description — "the Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao."
  • Wu wei (Non-doing): Not laziness but effortless action in harmony with nature — achieving through not forcing.
  • Yin and Yang: Opposing forces interdepend and cycle; wisdom is balance, not victory of one side.
  • Soft over Hard: Water, the softest thing, wears away stone. The yielding outlasts the rigid.
  • Return & Simplicity: The movement of the Dao is a return; the sage values plainness, emptiness, and the uncarved block.
