Epicurus

341 – 270 BCE · Samos & Athens
Epicureanism
Biography

Epicurus founded one of the most influential and misunderstood schools of antiquity. Born on the island of Samos, he settled in Athens and bought a house with a garden — the Garden — where men, women, and even slaves studied philosophy together, a radical egalitarianism for the time.

He taught that philosophy is a medicine for the soul. His writings were many, but only letters and fragments survive; our fullest source is the Roman poet Lucretius, whose "On the Nature of Things" expounds Epicurean physics with astonishing poetic power.

Contrary to later caricature, Epicurus did not advocate indulgence. He taught that the highest pleasure is the absence of pain and disturbance — a modest, friendly life attuned to nature and free from superstition.

Great Ideas
  • Ataraxia: The goal of life is tranquillity — a mind undisturbed by fear, desire, or false belief.
  • Death is Nothing: When we exist, death is not; when death exists, we are not. Therefore it is nothing to fear.
  • Atomism: The universe consists of atoms moving in a void. Even the soul is material and mortal, dissolving at death.
  • The Swerve: Atoms occasionally swerve without cause, allowing for free will in an otherwise deterministic cosmos.
  • Friendship: Of all the means to happiness, Epicurus prized friendship above even philosophy itself.
In His Words
"Death is nothing to us; for that which is dissolved is without sensation, and that which lacks sensation is nothing to us." — Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus
"Not what we have but what we enjoy, constitutes our abundance." — Epicurus
"Of all the things which wisdom provides to make life entirely happy, much the greatest is the possession of friendship." — Epicurus
