Baruch Spinoza

1632 – 1677 · Amsterdam & The Hague
Rationalism
Biography

Baruch Spinoza was born into the Jewish community of Amsterdam, the son of Portuguese refugees. His unorthodox views on God, scripture, and the soul led to his excommunication at age twenty-three — one of the harshest in the community's history.

He earned his living grinding lenses, an occupation that probably hastened his early death from lung disease. In modest rooms in The Hague he composed the "Ethics," a work of breathtaking rigor cast in geometrical form: definitions, axioms, propositions, and proofs.

His contemporaries called him an atheist, though he was perhaps the most God-intoxicated of philosophers. He identified God with Nature, rejected teleology, and proposed a vision of freedom as understanding necessity.

Great Ideas
  • Deus sive Natura: God is not a person outside the world but Nature itself — the infinite, self-caused substance of which everything is a mode.
  • Substance Monism: There is only one substance. Mind and body are not two things but two attributes of the same reality.
  • Determinism & Freedom: Everything follows necessarily from the divine nature. Freedom is not absence of causation but acting from one's own adequate understanding.
  • Conatus: Every finite thing strives to persevere in its being; this striving is the essence of the thing.
  • Three Kinds of Knowledge: Opinion, reason, and intuitive knowledge. The highest grasps things in their necessity and leads to blessedness.
In His Words
"God is the immanent, not the transitive, cause of all things." — Spinoza, Ethics
"The highest activity a human being can attain is learning for understanding, because to understand is to be free." — Spinoza
"I have made a ceaseless effort not to ridicule, not to bewail, not to scorn human actions, but to understand them." — Spinoza
