Martin Heidegger

1889 – 1976 · Germany
Phenomenology & Ontology
Biography

Martin Heidegger was one of the most influential and controversial philosophers of the twentieth century. Born in rural Germany, he became Husserl's assistant at Freiburg and succeeded him in the chair of philosophy.

His 1927 work "Being and Time" posed the question of the meaning of Being through an analysis of human existence, which he called Dasein. For a brief period he supported the Nazi regime, a moral failure that has shadowed his philosophical legacy ever since.

He spent his later years in the Black Forest, writing on technology, poetry, language, and the history of metaphysics. His critique of modernity's instrumental relation to the world remains deeply influential.

Great Ideas
  • Being-in-the-World: Human existence is not isolated consciousness but always already embedded in a meaningful world.
  • Dasein: The being for whom its own existence is an issue; Dasein is constituted by care, possibility, and temporality.
  • Being-Toward-Death: Authentic existence is grasped in the awareness of one's own finitude and the possibility of no-longer-being.
  • Thrownness & Authenticity: We are thrown into a world not of our choosing, yet we can take over our existence authentically.
  • The Question Concerning Technology: Modern technology reveals nature as a "standing-reserve" to be exploited, endangering our relation to Being.
In His Words
