Simone de Beauvoir

1908 – 1986 · Paris
Existentialism & Feminism
Biography

The first woman to earn the agrégation in philosophy in France, de Beauvoir was a formidable philosopher in her own right — not merely a companion to Sartre. She lived a life of intellectual independence, writing novels, memoirs, and treatises that reshaped how the world understands womanhood.

Her masterpiece, The Second Sex, is often called the founding text of second-wave feminism. She applied existentialism to the condition of women: if existence precedes essence, then women are not born but made into "the Other."

She insisted that liberation requires economic independence and that freedom is never a private possession — it is won only with and for others.

Great Ideas
  • "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman": Gender is not a fixed essence but a social becoming imposed by culture.
  • The Other: In patriarchy, man is the Subject and woman is defined as his lack, his opposite, his mystery.
  • Immanence vs Transcendence: Oppression traps one in mere repetition (immanence); freedom is the project of surpassing (transcendence).
  • Existential freedom for all: No one is free until everyone is — my freedom is bound up with yours.
  • Old Age & Death: She turned her clear gaze on aging and mortality, refusing comforting myths.
