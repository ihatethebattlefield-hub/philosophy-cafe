Moses Maimonides

1135 – 1204 · Córdoba & Cairo
Jewish Philosophy
Biography

Moses ben Maimon, known as Maimonides or Rambam, was the towering intellect of medieval Judaism. Born in Córdoba, he fled persecution in Al-Andalus and eventually became physician to the sultan's court in Cairo and head of the Jewish community of Egypt.

His "Guide for the Perplexed" sought to reconcile the Hebrew Bible and rabbinic tradition with Aristotelian philosophy. Written in Judeo-Arabic, it addressed a student troubled by apparent conflicts between reason and faith.

Maimonides also composed the Mishneh Torah, a comprehensive code of Jewish law, and medical treatises. His work shaped Jewish thought, influenced Thomas Aquinas, and remains central to contemporary Jewish philosophy.

Great Ideas
  • Negative Theology: We cannot affirm what God is; we can only say what God is not. Positive attributes are pedagogical, not literal.
  • The Golden Mean: Following Aristotle, moral virtue lies between extremes of excess and deficiency.
  • Faith & Reason: Philosophy and prophecy converge on the same truths, expressed differently for different audiences.
  • Providence & Evil: Divine providence extends to individuals according to their intellectual perfection; much suffering arises from natural necessity.
  • The Messianic Era: The age to come will be a political and moral restoration, not a supernatural disruption of nature.
