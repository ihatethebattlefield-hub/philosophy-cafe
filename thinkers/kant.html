Immanuel Kant

1724 – 1804 · Königsberg
German Idealism
Biography

Kant lived his entire life in Königsberg, kept a schedule so precise that neighbors set their clocks by his afternoon walks, and never married. Yet from this quiet provincial routine came a revolution in thought as profound as Copernicus's.

He awoke from what he called his "dogmatic slumber" and asked a deceptively simple question: how is knowledge possible at all? His answer — that the mind actively structures experience through categories of understanding — made the knowing subject, not the external world, the foundation of knowledge.

His three Critiques (of Pure Reason, Practical Reason, and Judgment) reshaped metaphysics, ethics, and aesthetics. He sought to reconcile the Enlightenment's faith in reason with a respect for morality and the limits of what we can know.

Great Ideas
  • The Categorical Imperative: Act only according to maxims you could will to become universal law. Treat humanity never merely as a means, but always as an end.
  • Transcendental Idealism: We know phenomena (things as they appear), never noumena (things-in-themselves). Space and time are forms our mind imposes.
  • Synthetic a priori: Some knowledge is both necessary (a priori) and informative about the world — mathematics and causality being prime examples.
  • Perpetual Peace: He sketched a vision of a federation of free states and a cosmopolitan right among peoples.
  • Autonomy: Moral worth comes from acting from duty freely chosen by a rational will, not from inclination.
In His Words
"Two things fill the mind with ever new and increasing admiration and awe… the starry heavens above me and the moral law within me." — Kant, Critique of Practical Reason
"Have the courage to use your own understanding — that is the motto of enlightenment." — Kant, What is Enlightenment?
"Out of the crooked timber of humanity, no straight thing was ever made." — Kant
