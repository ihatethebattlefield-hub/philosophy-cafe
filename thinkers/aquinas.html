Thomas Aquinas

1225 – 1274 · Italy & Paris
Scholasticism
Biography

Thomas Aquinas was a Dominican friar whose monumental synthesis of Aristotle and Christian theology earned him the title "Doctor Angelicus" and "Doctor Communis." Born into nobility, he scandalized his family by joining the mendicant Dominicans rather than the wealthier Benedictines.

He studied and taught in Cologne and Paris, producing the Summa Theologica and Summa contra Gentiles — works of breathtaking scope that sought to show how faith and reason, grace and nature, form a single coherent order.

Near the end of his life he reportedly had a mystical experience that caused him to call his writings "so much straw." He died at forty-nine, but his thought became the official philosophy of the Roman Catholic Church and a cornerstone of Western intellectual history.

Great Ideas
  • The Five Ways: Five arguments for the existence of God based on motion, causation, contingency, gradation, and final causality.
  • Natural Law: Moral principles are rooted in human nature and knowable by reason, complementing revealed divine law.
  • Faith & Reason: Grace does not destroy nature but perfects it. Reason and revelation are two paths toward one truth.
  • Substance & Accidents: Drawing on Aristotle, he distinguished what a thing is from its changeable qualities.
  • The Unmoved Mover: All change requires a first cause; this Pure Act is what Christians call God.
