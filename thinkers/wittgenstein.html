Ludwig Wittgenstein

1889 – 1951 · Austria & England
Analytic Philosophy
Biography

Ludwig Wittgenstein was born into one of the wealthiest families in Austro-Hungary but gave away his fortune and sought a life of intellectual austerity. He studied engineering, encountered the philosophy of mathematics, and went to Cambridge to work with Bertrand Russell.

His "Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus," written during the First World War, tried to show the limits of language and the nonsensicality of traditional metaphysics. Afterward he left philosophy, worked as a schoolteacher and an architect, only to return and revolutionize philosophy a second time.

His later work, "Philosophical Investigations," abandoned the idea of a single logical form of language in favor of countless "language games" embedded in forms of life. He remains a towering, enigmatic figure of twentieth-century thought.

Great Ideas
  • The Picture Theory: In the early work, propositions are logical pictures of possible states of affairs; meaning depends on correspondence to reality.
  • Language Games: Meaning is use. Words function differently in different practices — giving orders, praying, joking, doing mathematics.
  • Family Resemblance: Many concepts — like "game" — do not have a single defining feature but a network of overlapping similarities.
  • Private Language Argument: A language purely private to one individual is impossible; meaning requires public criteria and shared practices.
  • Philosophical Therapy: The task of philosophy is to dissolve conceptual confusions, not to construct grand theories.
In His Words
"The limits of my language mean the limits of my world." — Wittgenstein, Tractatus
"Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent." — Wittgenstein, Tractatus
"Philosophy is a battle against the bewitchment of our intelligence by means of language." — Wittgenstein, Investigations
