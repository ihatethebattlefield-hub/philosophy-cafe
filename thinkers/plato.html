Plato

c. 428 – 348 BCE · Athens
Founder of the Academy
Biography

Plato was a student of Socrates and the teacher of Aristotle — a triad that anchors nearly all of Western philosophy. Born into an aristocratic Athenian family, he witnessed the trial and execution of his beloved teacher Socrates, an event that seared into him a lifelong suspicion of democracy untempered by wisdom and a devotion to the examined life.

Around 387 BCE he founded the Academy in Athens, often described as the first institution of higher learning in the Western world. Above its entrance was said to be inscribed: "Let no one ignorant of geometry enter here." For four decades he wrote dialogues — dramatic conversations usually featuring Socrates — through which he explored justice, beauty, knowledge, and the soul.

Unlike most philosophers before him, Plato committed his thought to writing, yet he remained skeptical of the written word, fearing it could not answer back when questioned. His ideas shaped theology, science, politics, and mathematics for two millennia and continue to provoke us today.

Great Ideas
  • Theory of Forms: The physical world is only a shadow of a higher, eternal realm of perfect Forms or Ideas. A beautiful thing participates in Beauty itself; a just action participates in Justice itself.
  • The Allegory of the Cave: Humanity lives chained in a cave, mistaking shadows for reality. Education is the painful turning of the soul toward the light of the Forms.
  • The Philosopher-King: In the ideal city (The Republic), only those who have ascended to behold the Form of the Good are fit to rule.
  • The Tripartite Soul: Reason, spirit, and appetite must be harmonized — justice is each part doing its proper work.
  • Knowledge vs. Opinion: True knowledge (epistēmē) is justified, necessary, and of the unchanging; mere opinion (doxa) concerns the flux of the senses.
