Karl Marx

1818 – 1883 · Trier & London
Historical Materialism
Biography

Karl Marx was born in Trier, Prussia, into a middle-class Jewish family that converted to Christianity. He studied law and philosophy, became a radical journalist, and was repeatedly exiled until he settled in London, where he spent his most productive years in the reading room of the British Museum.

With Friedrich Engels, he wrote "The Communist Manifesto" and labored on "Capital," an analysis of how capitalism produces wealth and exploitation. He sought not merely to interpret the world but to change it.

His legacy is contested: regimes claiming his name committed atrocities, while his critique of capital continues to shape economics, sociology, and political movements around the globe.

Great Ideas
  • Historical Materialism: The economic base of society — its mode of production — shapes its politics, law, and culture.
  • Class Struggle: History is the history of class conflict; under capitalism, the struggle is between bourgeoisie and proletariat.
  • Alienation: Workers under capitalism are separated from the products of their labor, their own activity, their species-being, and one another.
  • Commodity Fetishism: Social relations between people appear as relations between things, hiding the human labor behind prices.
  • Ideology: Dominant ideas serve the interests of the ruling class, presenting contingent arrangements as natural and eternal.
In His Words
"The philosophers have only interpreted the world, in various ways. The point, however, is to change it." — Marx, Theses on Feuerbach
"Religion is the sigh of the oppressed creature, the heart of a heartless world." — Marx
"From each according to his ability, to each according to his needs." — Marx, Critique of the Gotha Program
