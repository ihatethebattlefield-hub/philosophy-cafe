John Stuart Mill

1806 – 1873 · England
Utilitarianism & Liberalism
Biography

John Stuart Mill was educated by his father James Mill and Jeremy Bentham in a rigorous experiment designed to produce a philosophical genius. He read Greek at three and Latin at eight, but suffered a mental crisis in early adulthood that taught him the limits of rational analysis alone.

He became a central figure in Victorian intellectual life, writing on logic, political economy, ethics, and women's rights. He served in Parliament and campaigned for female suffrage and colonial reform.

With Harriet Taylor, whom he later married, he refined utilitarianism into a liberal philosophy that prizes individuality, free expression, and the higher pleasures of intellect and imagination.

Great Ideas
  • The Liberty Principle: Society may restrict individual freedom only to prevent harm to others; paternalism is generally illegitimate.
  • Higher & Lower Pleasures: Not all pleasures are equal. It is better to be a human dissatisfied than a pig satisfied.
  • Rule Utilitarianism: We should follow rules that generally promote happiness, rather than calculating each act from scratch.
  • Marketplace of Ideas: Truth emerges from the collision of opposing opinions; censorship harms even the censor by silencing the truth.
  • Women's Equality: The subjection of women is a social artifact, not natural. Equality would benefit both sexes and society.
In His Words
"The only freedom which deserves the name is that of pursuing our own good in our own way." — Mill, On Liberty
"It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied." — Mill, Utilitarianism
"He who knows only his own side of the case knows little of that." — Mill, On Liberty
