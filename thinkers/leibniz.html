Gottfried Leibniz

1646 – 1716 · Leipzig, Hanover & Berlin
Rationalism
Biography

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz was a polymath who made lasting contributions to mathematics, logic, law, theology, and philosophy. Born in Leipzig, he taught himself from his father's library, earned a doctorate at twenty, and spent his career as a diplomat, librarian, and court adviser in Hanover.

Independently of Newton, he invented the differential and integral calculus and devised a notation still in use. Yet his deepest passion was a universal system of knowledge — a characteristica universalis that would reduce reasoning to calculation.

His last years were overshadowed by the controversy over calculus priority and neglect by his patron. He died in Hanover, largely alone, leaving thousands of unpublished manuscripts.

Great Ideas
  • Monads: The universe consists of simple, windowless substances — each a universe in miniature, mirroring the whole from its own point of view.
  • Best of All Possible Worlds: God chose to actualize the world containing the greatest possible perfection and harmony.
  • Principle of Sufficient Reason: Nothing exists or occurs without a reason why it is so rather than otherwise.
  • Pre-Established Harmony: Mind and body do not interact; each follows its own laws, synchronized by God from the beginning.
  • Possible Worlds: God surveys an infinity of possible worlds and creates the one whose overall goodness is maximal.
In His Words
