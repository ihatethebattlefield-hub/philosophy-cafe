Adi Shankara

c. 700 – 750 CE · India
Advaita Vedanta
Biography

Adi Shankara was a philosopher-saint who revived and systematized Advaita Vedanta, the non-dual interpretation of the Upanishads. Born in Kerala, he left home early, became a wandering monk, and traveled across the Indian subcontinent debating scholars and establishing monastic centers.

Though he lived only about thirty-two years, he wrote commentaries on the major Upanishads, the Bhagavad Gita, and the Brahma Sutras, as well as independent works such as the Vivekachudamani. His intellectual clarity and spiritual intensity reshaped Hindu philosophy.

Shankara insisted that liberation is not achieved by ritual or merit but by the direct knowledge that the individual self (Atman) is identical with the ultimate reality (Brahman).

Great Ideas
  • Non-Duality (Advaita): Reality is one without a second. The multiplicity of the world is not ultimately real; Brahman alone is.
  • Atman is Brahman: The true Self is not the body, mind, or ego but the infinite, pure consciousness identical with the Absolute.
  • Maya: The world of appearances is neither real nor unreal; it is superimposed upon Brahman like a snake upon a rope.
  • Jnana Yoga: Liberation (moksha) comes through knowledge — the discrimination between the real and the unreal.
  • Saguna & Nirguna Brahman: The Absolute can be approached with attributes for worship, yet it is ultimately without qualities.
In His Words
"Brahman is the only truth, the world is illusion, and there is ultimately no difference between Brahman and Atman." — Adi Shankara
"Do not be proud of wealth, people, relations and friends, or youth. All these are snatched by time in the blink of an eye." — Adi Shankara
"Knowledge is the only direct means to liberation." — Adi Shankara
