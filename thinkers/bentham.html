Jeremy Bentham

1748 – 1832 · England
Utilitarianism
Biography

Jeremy Bentham was a legal reformer, philosopher, and social engineer whose utilitarianism sought to make law, politics, and morals rational. Born in London, he trained as a lawyer but devoted himself to critiquing the irrationalities of the British legal system.

He designed the Panopticon, a model prison intended to reform inmates through surveillance; proposed schemes for public education, animal welfare, and poor relief; and left his body to science, now preserved as an "auto-icon" at University College London.

His relentless faith in measurement and reform earned him both admiration and mockery, yet few thinkers have done more to shape the modern welfare state and the moral landscape of policy-making.

Great Ideas
  • The Principle of Utility: The right action maximizes happiness and minimizes suffering for all affected.
  • The Felicific Calculus: A method for estimating pleasures and pains by intensity, duration, certainty, propinquity, fecundity, purity, and extent.
  • Legal Positivism: Law should be reformed according to utility rather than tradition or natural rights rhetoric.
  • Animal Suffering: The question is not whether animals can reason but whether they can suffer; their interests deserve moral weight.
  • Transparent Institutions: Public scrutiny and rational design can eliminate corruption and unnecessary suffering.
In His Words
"The greatest happiness of the greatest number is the foundation of morals and legislation." — Bentham
"Nature has placed mankind under the governance of two sovereign masters, pain and pleasure." — Bentham, Principles of Morals and Legislation
"The question is not, Can they reason? nor, Can they talk? but, Can they suffer?" — Bentham
