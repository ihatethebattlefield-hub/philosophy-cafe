Søren Kierkegaard

1813 – 1855 · Copenhagen
Existentialism & Christian Philosophy
Biography

Søren Kierkegaard was a Danish theologian, philosopher, and literary master whose writings gave birth to existentialism. The son of a melancholy merchant, he studied theology, broke a engagement that haunted him for life, and launched a literary career under a dazzling variety of pseudonyms.

Works such as "Either/Or," "Fear and Trembling," and "The Sickness Unto Death" explore anxiety, despair, faith, and the impossibility of becoming a self without God. He attacked the comfortable "Christendom" of his day for reducing faith to bourgeois respectability.

He collapsed in the street at forty-two and died shortly after. His thought would later inspire existentialists, theologians, and literary writers across the world.

Great Ideas
  • Stages on Life's Way: The aesthetic, ethical, and religious stages represent progressively deeper commitments to meaning.
  • The Leap of Faith: Faith is not a conclusion of reason but a passionate commitment beyond proof.
  • The Knight of Faith: Abraham's willingness to sacrifice Isaac exemplifies faith's terrifying paradox: the individual stands higher than the universal.
  • Subjective Truth: What matters is not merely what is true but how the truth is held — with passion, inwardness, and existence.
  • Anxiety & Freedom: Anxiety is the dizziness of freedom, the recognition that we can choose ourselves and our destinies.
