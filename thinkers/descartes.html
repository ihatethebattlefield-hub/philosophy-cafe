René Descartes

1596 – 1650 · France
Rationalism
Biography

René Descartes is often called the father of modern philosophy. Born in Touraine, France, he was educated by Jesuits, served as a soldier, and spent much of his adult life in the Dutch Republic, where he wrote in seclusion.

He sought to rebuild knowledge from certain foundations, sweeping away the inherited assumptions of scholasticism. His mathematical genius — he invented analytic geometry, uniting algebra and geometry — shaped his vision of a method that could bring clarity to every science.

He died in Stockholm while tutoring Queen Christina of Sweden. His project of radical doubt and his dualism of mind and body set the agenda for modern philosophy and continue to provoke debate.

Great Ideas
  • Cogito, Ergo Sum: "I think, therefore I am." Even in the deepest doubt, the thinking self is indubitably real.
  • Method of Doubt: Withhold belief from anything that can be doubted; accept only what is clear and distinct.
  • Mind-Body Dualism: The mind is a thinking, non-extended substance; the body is an extended, non-thinking substance.
  • Innate Ideas: Some truths — such as the idea of God and mathematical axioms — are not derived from experience but built into reason.
  • Cartesian Foundationalism: Knowledge should rest on indubitable first principles, like a chain secured at every link.
