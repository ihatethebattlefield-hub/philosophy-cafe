Michel Foucault

1926 – 1984 · France
Post-structuralism & History of Ideas
Biography

Michel Foucault was a French philosopher, historian, and social critic whose work reshaped the study of power, knowledge, and the body. Trained in psychology and philosophy, he held chairs at the Collège de France and lectured on topics ranging from ancient sexuality to modern penality.

Books such as "Madness and Civilization," "Discipline and Punish," and the multivolume "History of Sexuality" traced how modern institutions produce and regulate subjects. He was also an activist for prisoners' rights and victims of AIDS.

He died of AIDS-related illness in 1984, leaving behind one of the most provocative and cited bodies of work in the humanities.

Great Ideas
  • Power/Knowledge: Power and knowledge are inseparable; what counts as truth is shaped by regimes of power.
  • Discourse: Language is not neutral expression but a structured field that governs what can be said within particular disciplines.
  • Panopticism: Modern discipline works through internalized surveillance, making individuals regulate themselves.
  • Biopower: Modern states manage life itself — birth, death, health, sexuality — through scientific and administrative practices.
  • Subjectivation: We become who we are not simply by nature but through practices of self-formation and resistance.
In His Words
"Where there is power, there is resistance." — Foucault, The History of Sexuality
"The soul is the prison of the body." — Foucault, Discipline and Punish
"My point is not that everything is bad, but that everything is dangerous." — Foucault
