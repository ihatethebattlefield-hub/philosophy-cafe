William James

1842 – 1910 · New York & Massachusetts
Pragmatism & Psychology
Biography

William James was a psychologist, philosopher, and the leading figure of American pragmatism. Born into a brilliant family — his brother was the novelist Henry James — he struggled with depression and indecision before finding his vocation at Harvard.

His "Principles of Psychology" transformed the scientific study of mind, introducing the concept of the "stream of consciousness." Later he turned to philosophy of religion and metaphysics, arguing that beliefs should be judged by their fruits in experience.

James was a gifted lecturer and writer whose humane, pluralistic vision made philosophy speak to ordinary life. He died in 1910, leaving behind a body of work that remains a bridge between science and spirituality.

Great Ideas
  • Pragmatism: The meaning of an idea lies in its practical consequences. True ideas are those that work in experience.
  • The Stream of Consciousness: Thought is not a chain of ideas but a continuous, flowing stream, always changing and selecting.
  • The Will to Believe: In matters that cannot be decided by evidence alone, we have a right to believe what best supports a meaningful life.
  • Radical Empiricism: Experience includes relations as well as things. Reality is a pluralistic field of relations.
  • Pluralism: The universe is not a single closed system but a "multiverse" of overlapping experiences and purposes.
