Hannah Arendt

1906 – 1975 · Germany & USA
Political Philosophy
Biography

Hannah Arendt was a political thinker whose life was shaped by the catastrophes of the twentieth century. Born into a Jewish family in Germany, she studied with Martin Heidegger and Karl Jaspers before fleeing the Nazis in 1933.

Stateless for years, she eventually settled in the United States, where she wrote "The Origins of Totalitarianism," "The Human Condition," and "Eichmann in Jerusalem." Her report on the Eichmann trial introduced the controversial phrase "the banality of evil."

Arendt insisted on the dignity of politics, the fragility of public freedom, and the necessity of judgment. Her work remains essential for thinking about totalitarianism, revolution, and the public realm.

Great Ideas
  • The Banality of Evil: Great evil can arise not from monstrous motives but from thoughtlessness, conformity, and bureaucratic routine.
  • Action, Work, and Labor: Human activity can be distinguished into labor (life), work (world-making), and action (political speech and deed).
  • Public Realm: Freedom appears only in a public space of appearance where diverse citizens act together and are seen by one another.
  • Natality: The human capacity for beginning — being born and beginning something new — is the source of political hope.
  • Judgment: Political judgment requires the ability to think from the standpoint of others, an imaginative exercise of enlarged mentality.
In Her Words
"The most radical revolutionary will become a conservative the day after the revolution." — Arendt
"The sad truth is that most evil is done by people who never make up their minds to be good or evil." — Arendt
"Power is actualized only where word and deed have not parted company." — Arendt, The Human Condition
