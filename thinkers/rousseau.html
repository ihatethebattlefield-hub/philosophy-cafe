Jean-Jacques Rousseau

1712 – 1778 · Geneva & France
Social Contract & Romanticism
Biography

Jean-Jacques Rousseau was born in Geneva, the son of a watchmaker, and wandered through Europe as a musician, tutor, and adventurer before becoming one of the most provocative writers of the Enlightenment.

His "Discourse on the Origin of Inequality" and "The Social Contract" revolutionized political thought, while his novel "Emile" proposed a radical new education. His Confessions invented modern autobiography, exposing the self with unsparing candor.

Persecuted for his religious and political views, he spent his final years in relative isolation, prey to paranoia. Yet his influence on the French Revolution, Romanticism, and educational theory was immense.

Great Ideas
  • Natural Goodness: Human beings are naturally good; inequality, vice, and misery arise from corrupting social institutions.
  • The General Will: Legitimate sovereignty expresses the common good of all citizens, not merely the sum of private interests.
  • Social Contract: Individuals find moral freedom by submitting to a collective authority that protects equality and liberty.
  • Natural Education: The child should develop according to nature, not social convention; education must respect stages of growth.
  • Civil Religion: A society needs shared civic commitments to bind citizens together without tyrannizing conscience.
In His Words
"Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." — Rousseau, The Social Contract
"The first man who, having fenced in a piece of land, said 'This is mine,' was the true founder of civil society." — Rousseau, Discourse on Inequality
"People who know little are usually great talkers, while men who know much say little." — Rousseau
