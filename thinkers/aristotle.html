Aristotle

384 – 322 BCE · Stagira
The Peripatetic
Biography

Aristotle entered Plato's Academy at seventeen and remained for twenty years, until his teacher's death. Where Plato looked upward to a realm of perfect Forms, Aristotle kept his feet on the earth, insistently observing the natural world, classifying its creatures, and asking how things actually work.

He tutored Alexander the Great, founded his own school the Lyceum, and produced a body of work of staggering range — logic, biology, ethics, politics, poetics, physics, metaphysics. His writings were so systematic that later thinkers called him simply "the Philosopher."

His influence is hard to overstate: his logic framed scientific reasoning for two thousand years, his ethics shaped Christian and Islamic thought, and his emphasis on empirical observation helped seed the scientific revolution.

Great Ideas
  • The Golden Mean: Virtue lies between extremes — courage is the mean between recklessness and cowardice. Character is built by habit (ethos).
  • Teleology: Nature acts for ends. Every thing has a function, and its flourishing is the realization of its proper purpose (eudaimonia).
  • Form and Matter: Unlike Plato, he placed the Form within the thing — the oak is the oak-tree's form actualized in matter.
  • The Four Causes: To explain anything, give its material, formal, efficient, and final cause.
  • Logic & the Syllogism: He founded formal logic, showing how certain conclusions follow necessarily from premises.
In His Words
"We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an act, but a habit." — Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics
"It is the mark of an educated mind to be able to entertain a thought without accepting it." — Aristotle
"Man is by nature a political animal." — Aristotle, Politics
