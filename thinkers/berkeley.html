George Berkeley

1685 – 1753 · Ireland
Empiricism & Idealism
Biography

George Berkeley was an Irish philosopher, Anglican bishop, and missionary whose bold immaterialism challenged the foundations of both science and common sense. He studied at Trinity College Dublin and traveled widely, including an ambitious but ill-fated plan to found a college in Bermuda.

His early works — "A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge" and "Three Dialogues" — argued that matter, as conceived by Locke, does not exist. The only realities are minds and their ideas.

Berkeley spent his later years as Bishop of Cloyne, writing on economics, medicine, and the tar-water remedy. His idealism was ridiculed by his contemporaries but remains a live option in philosophy of mind.

Great Ideas
  • Esse est Percipi: "To be is to be perceived." The existence of sensible things consists in their being perceived by a mind.
  • Immaterialism: There is no matter independent of perception. Reality is a system of ideas sustained by spirit.
  • God as Divine Perceiver: The continued existence of the world when humans do not perceive it is guaranteed by God's all-encompassing perception.
  • Critique of Abstract Ideas: Locke's general ideas are impossible; we can only think of particular ideas used as signs.
  • Visual Language: The regularity of nature is a divine language: God speaks to us through the orderly succession of ideas.
