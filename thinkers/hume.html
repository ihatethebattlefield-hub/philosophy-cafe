David Hume

1711 – 1776 · Scotland
Empiricism & Skepticism
Biography

David Hume was the most influential British philosopher of the eighteenth century. Born in Edinburgh, he studied law, traveled in France, and wrote his masterpiece, "A Treatise of Human Nature," before the age of thirty.

The Treatise famously "fell dead-born from the press," but Hume recast its arguments in more accessible essays and the "Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding." He also wrote a bestselling history of England and was regarded as a genial, portly figure in Edinburgh society.

His skeptical examinations of causation, the self, and religion provoked charges of atheism, yet his style remained urbane and his curiosity boundless. Adam Smith called him as near to perfection as any human could be.

Great Ideas
  • The Problem of Induction: No amount of observed instances can logically guarantee that the future will resemble the past.
  • Causation as Custom: We cannot perceive necessary connection; causal belief arises from repeated experience and habit of mind.
  • The Is-Ought Gap: One cannot derive a moral obligation from a mere statement of fact; a fallacy lurks in many ethical arguments.
  • The Bundle Theory of Self: The self is not a substance but a bundle of perceptions in perpetual flux.
  • Skeptical Empiricism: All ideas derive from impressions, and many traditional philosophical questions dissolve when this principle is rigorously applied.
In His Words
"Reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions." — Hume, Treatise
"A wise man proportions his belief to the evidence." — Hume, Enquiry
"Be a philosopher; but, amidst all your philosophy, be still a man." — Hume
