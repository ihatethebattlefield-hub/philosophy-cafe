Mary Wollstonecraft

1759 – 1797 · England
Feminism & Political Philosophy
Biography

Mary Wollstonecraft was a writer, translator, and political philosopher who laid the philosophical foundations of modern feminism. Born into a financially unstable family, she supported herself by writing, teaching, and working as a governess.

Her "A Vindication of the Rights of Woman" argued that women are not naturally inferior but are made so by deficient education and social expectations. She demanded equal rational education and moral dignity for women.

Her life was as bold as her thought: she traveled alone, had a child out of wedlock, and married the philosopher William Godwin shortly before dying from complications of childbirth. She left a legacy that inspired generations of reformers.

Great Ideas
  • Rights of Women: Women possess the same rational souls and natural rights as men; justice demands their equal recognition.
  • Education & Equality: The apparent weakness of women is produced by miseducation. Equal schooling would reveal equal capacity.
  • Virtue for All: Moral virtue cannot flourish without freedom and independence; dependence corrupts both sexes.
  • Critique of Sensibility: The culture of feminine sentimentalism weakens women by valuing feeling over reason.
  • Social Reform: The improvement of women is inseparable from the improvement of society as a whole.
In Her Words
"I do not wish them to have power over men; but over themselves." — Wollstonecraft, Rights of Woman
"If women be educated for dependence, that is, to act according to the will of another fallible being, they cannot be consistently virtuous." — Wollstonecraft
"Virtue can only flourish among equals." — Wollstonecraft
