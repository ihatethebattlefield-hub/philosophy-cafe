Jean-Paul Sartre

1905 – 1980 · Paris
Existentialism
Biography

Sartre was the public face of twentieth-century existentialism — a philosopher, novelist, and playwright who wrote in Parisian cafés and marched in the streets. He refused the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1964, declaring a writer must not let himself be "institutionalized."

With his lifelong companion Simone de Beauvoir, he made philosophy a lived practice: engaged, political, and inseparable from freedom. His famous lecture "Existentialism is a Humanism" tried to answer those who feared his philosophy bred despair.

For Sartre, there is no human nature given in advance. We are condemned to be free, to choose, and in choosing for ourselves we choose an image of what humanity should be.

Great Ideas
  • Existence precedes essence: We first exist, then define ourselves through choices. Humans are not born with a fixed nature.
  • Radical freedom: We are "condemned to be free" — there is no escape from the burden of choosing.
  • Bad faith (mauvaise foi): The self-deception of hiding from our freedom by pretending we are merely what others or roles make us.
  • Being-for-itself vs Being-in-itself: Consciousness is a lack, a nothingness that distances us from the solid things of the world.
  • Engaged literature: Writing is an appeal to the freedom of the reader; the author and reader co-create meaning.
