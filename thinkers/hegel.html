G.W.F. Hegel

1770 – 1831 · Germany
German Idealism
Biography

Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel was the last of the great German idealists. Born in Stuttgart, he studied theology, worked as a tutor and newspaper editor, and eventually became professor of philosophy in Berlin, where his lectures drew students from across Europe.

His major works — the "Phenomenology of Spirit," the "Science of Logic," and the "Elements of the Philosophy of Right" — develop a system of extraordinary ambition in which history, art, religion, and philosophy progressively disclose the nature of freedom.

He died of cholera in 1831, leaving disciples who divided into radical and conservative camps. Whether he is read as an apologist for the Prussian state or a prophet of revolutionary change, his influence on Marx, Kierkegaard, and twentieth-century thought is immeasurable.

Great Ideas
  • The Dialectic: Thought and history advance through thesis, antithesis, and synthesis, each stage preserving and transcending the previous.
  • Absolute Spirit: Reality is the self-unfolding of Spirit, coming to know itself through nature, history, art, religion, and philosophy.
  • Master-Slave Dialectic: Self-consciousness emerges through recognition; the struggle for dominance reveals the dependence of master on slave.
  • History as Progress of Freedom: "The history of the world is none other than the progress of the consciousness of freedom."
  • Sittlichkeit: True ethics are lived within the institutions of family, civil society, and the state, not merely abstract moral choice.
In His Words
"The real is rational and the rational is real." — Hegel, Philosophy of Right
"Nothing great in the world has ever been accomplished without passion." — Hegel
"To be independent of public opinion is the first formal condition of achieving anything great." — Hegel
