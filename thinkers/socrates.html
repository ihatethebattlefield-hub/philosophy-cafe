Socrates

c. 470 – 399 BCE · Athens
Classical Greek Philosophy
Biography

Socrates is the philosopher who wrote nothing — and changed everything. He spent his days in the Athenian agora, the marketplace, questioning citizens about courage, justice, piety, and wisdom. His method was not to lecture but to draw out contradictions in what people believed they knew.

He fought as a hoplite in Athens' wars, raised a family, and became both admired and resented for exposing the limits of powerful men. In 399 BCE he was charged with impiety and corrupting the youth, convicted by a narrow margin, and executed by hemlock. He refused to escape or recant, dying as he had lived: faithful to the examined life.

We know him chiefly through his students Plato and Xenophon, and through the comic portrait of Aristophanes. Yet the figure that emerges — gadfly, midwife, moral hero — has made him the archetype of the philosopher in the West.

Great Ideas
  • The Socratic Method: Knowledge is tested through question and answer. By exposing inconsistencies, interlocutors are led to clearer, more defensible beliefs.
  • "Know Thyself": The Delphic inscription became his motto. The unexamined assumptions of a life are the first things philosophy must confront.
  • Virtue is Knowledge: If people truly understood the good, they would do it. Wrongdoing stems from ignorance, not a will to harm.
  • Socratic Ignorance: Aware of his own lack of wisdom, he claimed only to know that he did not know — a humility that opened the path to genuine inquiry.
  • The Examined Life: A life without self-questioning is not worth living. Philosophy is not a doctrine but a practice of waking up.
