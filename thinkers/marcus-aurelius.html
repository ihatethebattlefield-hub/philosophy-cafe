Marcus Aurelius

121 – 180 CE · Rome
Stoicism
Biography

Marcus Aurelius was the last of Rome's so-called Five Good Emperors, ruling from 161 to 180 CE. Adopted into the imperial family and educated in rhetoric, law, and philosophy, he turned especially to Stoicism under the tutelage of Junius Rusticus.

His "Meditations" were written in Greek during military campaigns along the Danube frontier. They were private notes, never intended for publication — a ruler reminding himself how to meet plague, war, betrayal, and death with equanimity.

He embodies the rare union of power and philosophy. Though he persecuted Christians as a threat to Roman order, his writings remain a touchstone for anyone seeking inner discipline amid external chaos.

Great Ideas
  • Dichotomy of Control: Some things are within our power — our judgments and choices; most are not. Serenity comes from distinguishing the two.
  • Memento Mori: Remember that you must die. Awareness of mortality strips away pettiness and clarifies what truly matters.
  • Cosmic Reason (Logos): The universe is ordered by a rational principle; to resist it is to suffer, to accept it is to live in harmony.
  • Inner Citadel: No one can disturb the soul without its consent. The mind can remain free even when the body is constrained.
  • Duty to the Whole: We are parts of a larger social and cosmic body. Justice and service are expressions of our nature.
In His Words
"You have power over your mind — not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations
"Waste no more time arguing about what a good man should be. Be one." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations
"Everything we hear is an opinion, not a fact. Everything we see is a perspective, not the truth." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations
