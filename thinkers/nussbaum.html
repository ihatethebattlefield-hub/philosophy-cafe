Martha Nussbaum

1947– · United States
Liberalism & Ethics
Biography

Martha Nussbaum is one of the most prominent moral and political philosophers of our time. Born in New York, she trained in classics and philosophy at Harvard, where she studied under G.E.L. Owen and John Rawls. She is the Ernst Freund Distinguished Service Professor at the University of Chicago.

Her work ranges across ancient Greek and Roman philosophy, ethics, political theory, feminism, and the philosophy of emotions. She has been a tireless advocate for global justice, disability rights, and the ethical treatment of animals.

Nussbaum combines rigorous analysis with humane concern, insisting that philosophy must engage with the concrete conditions of human flourishing around the world.

Great Ideas
  • The Capabilities Approach: A just society enables its members to develop central human capabilities — from health and education to political voice and emotional well-being.
  • Moral Emotions: Emotions are not obstacles to reason but appraisals of what matters; compassion and anger have moral roles.
  • Human Dignity: Every person has intrinsic worth and should be treated as an end, not merely a resource for economic growth.
  • Cosmopolitanism: Our moral obligations extend across national borders to all human beings, not only to fellow citizens.
  • Fragility of Goodness: Human flourishing is vulnerable to fortune; ethical life must acknowledge luck, loss, and dependence.
In Her Words
"To be a good human being is to have a kind of openness to the world, an ability to trust uncertain things beyond your own control." — Nussbaum
"Emotions are not just feelings; they are appraisals of things that matter." — Nussbaum
"The good human life is one in which the person has available to her the major opportunities for choice." — Nussbaum
