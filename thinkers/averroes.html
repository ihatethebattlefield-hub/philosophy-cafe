Averroes (Ibn Rushd)

1126 – 1198 · Córdoba, Al-Andalus
Aristotelianism
Biography

Ibn Rushd, called Averroes in the Latin West, was born into a distinguished family of jurists in Córdoba. He served as physician and judge, wrote commentaries on nearly all of Aristotle's works, and defended philosophy against the theological criticisms of al-Ghazali.

His "Incoherence of the Incoherence" argued that reason and revelation, properly understood, cannot conflict. In medieval Europe he became simply "the Commentator," second in authority only to Aristotle himself.

Political fortunes shifted: he was briefly banished and his books burned, yet his influence endured in Jewish and Christian scholastic thought, where debates about his view of the intellect continued for centuries.

Great Ideas
  • Harmony of Faith & Reason: True philosophy and true scripture agree. Where they seem to conflict, scripture should be interpreted allegorically.
  • Unity of the Intellect: There is one shared Agent Intellect; individual humans participate in it, achieving immortality through the knowledge they attain.
  • Eternity of the World: The cosmos has no temporal beginning; creation ex nihilo is philosophically untenable though not demonstrable.
  • Demonstrative Truth: The highest form of knowledge proceeds by syllogistic proof, accessible to philosophers.
  • Law & Philosophy: Religious law contains symbols of philosophical truths; philosophers interpret these symbols for the elite.
In His Words
"Ignorance leads to fear, fear leads to hate, and hate leads to violence. This is the equation." — Averroes
"Philosophy is the friend and milk-sister of religion." — Averroes
"Knowledge is the conformity of the object and the intellect." — Averroes
