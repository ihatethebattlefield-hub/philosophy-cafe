John Locke

1632 – 1704 · England
Empiricism & Liberalism
Biography

John Locke was a physician, political theorist, and philosopher whose ideas helped shape the Enlightenment and modern liberal democracy. Born in Somerset, he studied medicine at Oxford and later became adviser to the Earl of Shaftesbury.

His "Essay Concerning Human Understanding" attacked innate ideas and argued that all knowledge arises from experience. His "Two Treatises of Government" defended limited government, natural rights, and the right of revolution.

Exiled during the reign of James II, Locke returned after the Glorious Revolution and spent his final years writing on education, toleration, and the conduct of the understanding.

Great Ideas
  • Tabula Rasa: The mind at birth is a blank slate; all ideas come from sensation or reflection.
  • Primary & Secondary Qualities: Bodies have objective qualities like shape and motion, while colors, sounds, and tastes exist only in the perceiver.
  • Natural Rights: Individuals possess rights to life, liberty, and property independent of government.
  • Social Contract: Government derives its legitimacy from the consent of the governed and exists to protect natural rights.
  • Toleration: The state should not coerce religious belief; faith is a matter of individual conscience.
In His Words
"The mind is a blank slate, written on by experience." — Locke, Essay Concerning Human Understanding
"All mankind... being all equal and independent, no one ought to harm another in his life, health, liberty, or possessions." — Locke, Second Treatise
"New opinions are always suspected, and usually opposed, without any other reason but because they are not already common." — Locke
